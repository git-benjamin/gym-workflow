## TL;DR

You're 23 weeks into a 20.3 kg cut (148.9 → 128.6 kg, **0.88 kg/week**) on
retatrutide while training hard and protecting LBM. The cut is **well

(RHR net **−4 bpm**, HRV trending up), OSA is **clinically resolved on

chronic short sleep**. 43 % of nights are under 6 h; Tue–Thu average
5 h 50 m. This sits on top of a permanent AM stimulant load (Vyvanse 50 mg)
and an evening training schedule (9–10 pm). It's the single modifiable

appetite regulation.

The other watch items are smaller: rapid loss rate brushes the
gallstone-risk threshold for crash dieting; bloody-stool history on the
fibre ramp warrants the stool screen already on the GP ask list; reta
sourcing risk (failing COAs) is what's driving the tirzepatide switch.

## 4. Sleep

| Metric | Pre-reta | Current |
|---|---|---|
| Avg duration | 6 h 03 m | 6 h 16 m |
| Sleep score | 57 | 66 |
| Deep + REM | 1 h 44 m | 2 h 12 m |
| Efficiency | 94.8 % | 96.5 % |

**Duration is structurally short and reta hasn't moved it.** 43 % of nights
under 6 h, 74 % under 7 h. **Quality has improved meaningfully** (+9 pts
score, +28 min deep+REM) — almost certainly weight loss easing the OSA
burden (consistent with the AHI < 1 finding).

**Two structural patterns in the data:**

- **Late training costs ~26 min/night.** Training nights 6 h 00 m vs rest
  nights 6 h 26 m. Same deep+REM, same efficiency — you fall asleep later
  but sleep just as well once asleep. At 4–5 training nights/week that's
  100–130 min/week of lost duration. Reclaimable if any earlier
  training-time experimentation is feasible.
- **Tue–Thu are the bottleneck** (avg 5 h 50 m vs Fri–Sun 6 h 36 m).
  Lines up with the high-frequency block pattern flagged in the profile
  (the April 10-day streak as evidence). Saturday night (6 h 46 m) is the
  only night that meaningfully recovers.

**The Vyvanse interaction is the elephant in the room.** Lisdexamfetamine
half-life is ~10–12 h; the active dexamphetamine metabolite has its own
~10 h half-life. A 50 mg AM dose taken at, say, 07:00 still has
measurable plasma levels at 21:00 — exactly the late-training and
sleep-onset window. The dataset doesn't disprove this (we don't have
biomarker timing), but it's a coherent explanation for why the duration
floor is so stubborn even as the OSA-side metrics improve.
